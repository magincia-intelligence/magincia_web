## Top of the brief

- **Clare at Australia-India leaders' meeting: Flinders University joins eight approved for India campuses; Maitri grants fund 41 projects at $10m in 2026 (9 July).** At the bilateral meeting between PM Albanese and PM Modi, Australia and India signed the Memorandum of Understanding for the Australia-Canada-India Technology and Innovation (ACITI) Partnership on critical and emerging technologies. On the education and skills side, Flinders University received approval to establish a campus in India, joining seven other Australian universities already endorsed to operate there. Leaders noted the growing VET dimension, with a WA TAFE agreement to support a Centre of Excellence for Skilling in Mining and Mining Equipment, Technology and Services in India. India is Australia's second-largest source market with over 140,000 Indian students studying in Australian institutions last year. Clare: "Education is one of the most important bridges between Australia and India. It's not a one way street. It's not just about Indian students studying in Australia. It's increasingly about Australian universities taking the best of Australian education to India." ([Clare, 9 July](https://jasonclare.com.au/media/advancing-education-science-and-technology-ties-with-india/))

## Funding & system architecture

- **Norton: Universities Accord Bill's needs-based funding contains SES-address flaw that will misclassify relocating students; ministerial guidelines carry no legislative standing (9 July).** In the fourth part of his "proposed new funding system" series, Andrew Norton examines the bill's equity and regional components: $1,535 per commencing low-SES or Indigenous student and $1,398 per commencing regional student, with continuing students measured via EFTSL. He identifies a "certain step backwards" from current practice: the bill defines low-SES eligibility at "the time the person enrols in the unit," so students who relocate to study adopt addresses near universities — which ABS classifies as high-SES — and lose eligibility for subsequent enrolments. Norton proposes redrafting to reference "first enrolled at the provider." He further notes that the Needs-based Funding Grants Guidelines, which govern equity modifiers and regional modifiers, carry no parliamentary standing — making future downward adjustment structurally easy under budget pressure — and that needs-based payments are not reduced if a university has exceeded its ATEC allocation cap. ([Norton, 9 July](https://andrewnorton.id.au/2026/07/09/the-proposed-new-funding-system-part-4-needs-based-funding/))

## International education

- **IIE: 59% of US colleges record falling international applications; F-1 issuance to Indian applicants down 62%; Germany emerging as direct competitor for Australia's India pipeline (8 July).** The Institute of International Education's 2026 Spring Snapshot (585 institutions) records 59% of US colleges experiencing application declines for 2026-27, with 63% anticipating further falls and nearly one in four expecting a "substantial decline." F-1 visa issuance to Indian applicants fell 62% last year; over 60% of institutions saw falling Indian applications. Germany is described as "the clearest winner" among alternative destinations — Indian student interest has doubled since 2022, with visa approval rates of 90–95% achievable in as few as six working days. The Australian read-across is direct: India is Australia's second-largest source market and faces a 42% visa refusal rate (early 2026); Germany's processing speed and reliability advantage now competes in the same demand pool that Australia is attempting to hold under the 2027 NPL of 295,000. ([The PIE, 8 July](https://thepienews.com/60-of-us-colleges-report-fall-in-international-applications/))
