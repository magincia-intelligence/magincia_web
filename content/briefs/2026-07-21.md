## Top of the brief

- **Norton: minister — not ATEC — controls Commonwealth supported places for 179 non-Table A providers; six small providers face over-enrolment buffers of fewer than three places (20 July).** In the sixth instalment of his Universities Accord Bill series, Andrew Norton examines how the new funding system applies to five private universities, eight university colleges, and 166 other TEQSA-registered higher education providers. The central structural finding is ministerial control: the minister, not ATEC, determines which non-Table A providers receive CSPs, and ATEC can only allocate within "priority class of course" categories specified by the minister — enabling course-level micromanagement unavailable for public universities. The 2026 non-Table A CSP allocation totals $39.3 million against $7.8 billion for Table A public universities; "no major non-Table A growth expected." Six providers face over-enrolment buffers of fewer than three places, making financial penalties potentially existential relative to their CSP revenue. Norton argues that including Table B private universities in mission-based compacts is "completely inappropriate" given they receive no Commonwealth funding and should provide genuine independent alternatives to the public system. ([Norton, 20 July](https://andrewnorton.id.au/2026/07/20/the-proposed-new-funding-system-part-6-private-universities-and-non-university-higher-education-providers/))

## International education

- **Nepal's Cabinet approves legally binding regulations for education consultancies effective 8 July; ECAN first vice-president warns rules "will completely ruin the sector" as 123,000 no-objection certificates were issued for overseas study in 2025 (20 July).** The new framework mandates an NPR 2.5 million (~£12,000) security deposit, annual licence renewals, property ownership or a three-year lease, government approval for overseas education fairs, a mandatory grading system, and increased financial liability for agencies. Bashu Dev Dahal of the Educational Consultancy Association of Nepal: "This regulation will completely ruin the sector." Australia is among Nepal's leading study destinations alongside Japan, the US, Canada, and the UK; contraction in viable consultancy capacity adds a supply-side risk to a pipeline already constrained by a 69% Australian visa refusal rate for Nepali applicants. ([The PIE, 20 July](https://thepienews.com/nepalese-education-consultancies-protest-sweeping-new-regulations/))
